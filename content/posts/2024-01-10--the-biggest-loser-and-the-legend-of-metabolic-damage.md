## The _Biggest Loser_ and CICO

_The Biggest Loser_ was (is?) a popular Reality TV show, wherein number of contestants, all morbidly obese, are sent to a controlled environment where they are forced to eat less and move more. This generally involves challenges, conflict, screaming, histrionics, genuine emotion, amazing transformations, etc.

The show has been criticized on a number of grounds, but that's not relevant at this time. The core idea of the show, its fundamental assumption, is that by increasing physical activity ("calories out") and/or decreasing consumption ("calories in"), one can create a caloric deficit. In line with basic laws of thermodynamics and our understanding of the human body, this will lead to weight loss.

This core idea, often referred to as CICO ("Calories In-Calories Out"), has encountered some resistance in various circles. For a time, I was fascinated by a certain percentage of the population that rejected this idea entirely, saying that the reality of weight and weight loss is more complicated. When asked _how_ it is more complicated, they tend to gesture vaguely at gut flora or other hot topics in medical research, or simply deflect and say it's actually more healthy to learn to love and accept your body than to undergo a diet.

To be clear, losing weight is incredibly difficult in just about every way. I've struggled with my weight for most of my adult life. I'm not critiquing the idea of fatphobia here, or obesogenic culture, or the obstacles and hindrances and difficulties that confront people who want to lose weight. But I do think, ultimately, that it _is_ as simple as CICO, and that to say otherwise is, basically, Flat-Earth level resistance to science.

## The _Biggest Loser_ Study

Now, the important part of this story begins in 2016, when [this article](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/epdf/10.1002/oby.21538) was published. The conclusion drawn from this study is that, surprisingly, six years after appearing on _The Biggest Loser_, contestants' metabolisms showed evidence of having been "damaged"; their resting metabolic rate (RMR) was lower than would be expected:

>Mean RMR after 6 years was ~500 kcal/day lower than expected based on the measured body composition changes and the increased age of the subjects.

The lesson would seem to be that a drastic diet and exercise plan might actually permanently lower your resting metabolic rate by an incredible amount. This would obviously make it incredibly difficult to maintain any sort of normal, healthy weight.

I want to emphasize that 500 calories per day is an absolutely incredible amount for a resting metabolic rate to change. That's the difference between maintaining a weight and gaining (or losing) one pound per week!

Of course, this made the news; I saw it on dozens of websites. A lot of the comments were convinced that this study had proven that there was no benefit to lifestyle modification; what's the point, they asked, if I'm just going to end up gaining it all back and then being even fatter?

Here are a couple:

>I was not expecting that when they regained the weight, their metabolism was slower than before they ever started exercising. That just seems unfair. 

>I find it so depressing that if I want to be thin, I have to be hungry, all the time.

This finding seemed counterintuitive to me, and I was deeply interested in the subject, so I read the paper and tried my best to understand the methodology.

## A Flawed Methodology, or "You get metabolic damage! And you get metabolic damage!"

>Baseline data from all 16 subjects were used to generate a least squares best-fit linear regression equation for RMR as a function of FFM, FM, age, and sex (R^2=0.84):

>>RMR(kcal/d) = 1,001 + 21.2 * FFM(kg) + 1.4 * FM(kg) - 7.1 * Age(years) + 276 * Sex(F = 0, M = 1)

>We calculated the predicted RMR using this equation along with the corresponding FFM, FM, and age at each time point for every individual. Differences between the measured and predicted RMR defined the magnitude of metabolic adaptation which was considered to be present if the RMR residuals were significantly different from zero.

In other words, the researchers:

1. measured the actual RMR of the subjects
2. used statistical methods to create a formula for the subjects' RMR
3. measured the actual RMR of the subjects six years later
4. used the RMR formula to predict the RMR of the subjects
5. the discrepancy between predicted RMR and actual RMR would be reported as metabolic damage.

How does their RMR formula stack up against an established standard formula that is found to hold true for wide swaths of the population, like the Mifflin-St. Jeor formula?

> MSJ RMR(kcal/d) = 10 * Weight(kg) + 6.25 * Height(cm) - 5 * Age(years) + (F = -161, M = 5)

It's tracking somewhat different variables -- for instance, Mifflin-St. Jeor tracks overall weight and height, whereas the "Biggest Loser" equation tracks fat-free mass and fat mass. I think it's interesting that the sex difference leads to about about 100 calories more for men under the "Biggest Loser" study than under the Mifflin-St. Jeor equation.

Now, it's important to note that the researchers mention the Mifflin-St. Jeor formula:

>Despite all our subjects having class III obesity at baseline, the coefficients of the best-fit RMR regression equation above were similar to those previously published using data from subjects with less severe obesity (11-13). Furthermore, the baseline RMR measurements in our subjects were not significantly different (P=0.34).

Let's look at those equations:

>On the basis of literature values, the relationship between fat-free mass (FFM), fat mass (FM), and resting energy expenditure [REE (kJ/24 h)] was determined for 213 adults (86 males, 127 females). The objectives were to develop a mathematical model to predict REE based on body composition and to evaluate the contribution of FFM and FM to REE. The following regression equations were derived: 1) REE = 1265 + (93.3 x FFM) (r2 = 0.727, P < 0.001); 2) REE = 1114 + (90.4 x FFM) + (13.2 x FM) (R2 = 0.743, P < 0.001); and 3) REE = (108 x FFM) + (16.9 x FM) (R2 = 0.986, P < 0.001). FM explained only a small part of the variation remaining after FFM was accounted for. The models that include both FFM and FM are useful in examination of the changes in REE that occur with a change in both the FFM and FM. To account for more of the variability in REE, FFM will have to be divided into organ mass and skeletal muscle mass in future analyses.
>-[Prediction of resting energy expenditure from fat-free mass and fat mass](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1415003/)

>Differences in body composition have often been examined in conjunction with measurements of energy expenditure in men and women. Numerous studies during the past decade examined the relationship between resting energy expenditure (REE) and the components of a two-compartment model of composition, namely the fat-free mass (FFM) and the fat mass (FM). A synthetic review of these studies confirms a primary correlation between REE and FFM in adults over a broad range of body weights. A generalized prediction equation is proposed as REE = 370 +/- 21.6 x FFM. This equation explains 65-90% of the variation in REE. Several studies suggest, further, that FFM predicts total daily energy expenditure (TDEE) equally well. An independent contribution by FM to the prediction of either REE or TDEE is not supported for the general population, perhaps reflecting the relative constancy of the absolute FM in nonobese individuals. In the subset of obese women, FM may be a significant predictor.
>– [Body composition as a determinant of energy expenditure: a synthetic review and a proposed general prediction equation](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1957828/)

So we have the following formulae:

>RMR = (1265 + (93.3 x FFM)) * 0.2390057
>RMR = (1114 + (90.4 x FFM) + (13.2 x FM)) * 0.2390057
>RMR = ((108 x FFM) + (16.9 x FM)) * 0.2390057

(These have a multiplier to convert from kJ to kcal)

>RMR = 370 + 21.6 x FFM (the "Cunningham Formula")

Let's take a 43-year-old, 5'9" man who weighs 80kg and has a body fat percentage of 15%. Under the Cunningham formula, he has an RMR of ~1838. The three formulae above put that at ~1818, ~1773, and ~1803. The "Biggest Loser" formula puts it at ~2430. MSJ puts it at ~1680.

Let's take a 28-year-old, 5'3" woman who weighs 148kg and has a body fat percentage of 50%. Under the Cunningham formula, she has an RMR of ~1968. The three formulae prior put it at ~1952, ~2098, and ~2209. The "Biggest Loser" formula puts it at ~2475. MSJ puts it at ~2200.

I could keep going, but I think this demonstrates my point: the "Biggest Loser" formula seems to overestimate RMR relative to other, accepted formulae, specifically those cited as points of comparison by the researchers in question. It would seem that the example man might have 630 calories of "metabolic damage," and the example woman might have 200-400.

I invite you, dear reader, to substitute your own data into these formulae and see how little or how large their disagreement might be.

## The Point

There is no indication that the "Biggest Loser" participants actually suffered any decrease in their RMR. The study researchers could have bolstered their case by showing predictions and discrepancies under the Mifflin-St. Jeor formula, but they did not, perhaps because no other formula actually produced a discrepancy.
