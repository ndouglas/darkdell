As mentioned [previously](/posts/2024-01-09--job-seeking-and-its-discontents/), I received a bit of a rude surprise at work; our contract with the client was ending unexpectedly, and there was a risk that I might be laid off. Shortly after I started that search, my employer offered me a position on another team, which I gratefully accepted. Crisis averted.

However, I'd already fired off a number of job applications. And the responses (when they came at all) weren't particularly pleasant. I was ghosted, I was rejected, etc. Got a few interviews, but fewer than I would've expected. (I'm not sure how far those would've gone; I didn't want to waste anyone's time, so I withdrew my applications once I was offered my now-current position.)

Also mentioned previously is that I now find myself with three versions of my résumé, and I'm happy with none of them. So, per my [usual model](https://xkcd.com/927/), I'm creating a fourth, and just to make things really frustrating, I'm doing it in LaTeX.
<!-- more -->
When I apply for a job, I'm quite confident I can excel at it. I'm too anxious of a person to willingly get in over my head. Onboarding to a new project and with new teammates is difficult enough without also having to onboard to novel concepts.

So if a job requires Chef experience, I won't apply; I have no Chef experience, and I'm not interested in misrepresenting myself or my skills. But if it requires IaC experience and mentions I'd be working with Chef, I might very well apply; I have enough experience with IaC via Ansible, Terraform, CloudFormation, SAM, etc, that I'm _very_ confident I could quickly be productive with Chef. On the other hand, I wouldn't apply for an embedded programming job, because that would be effectively new and it might take me some time to be even minimally competent.

And although it's not a major part of my point, I should mention that I always write a targeted cover letter when I apply for a job. Look, I _love_ talking about myself. I think I'm weird af. And I love talking about technology. Give me someone's complete and undivided attention and a writing prompt (even in the form of a job description) and I'll write some crap.

This leads me to conclude one of a few things:
- my self-representation via my résumé and cover letter is broken, or
- the hiring process (across multiple employers and systems) is broken, or
- some combination of both

I know it's a tough market out there for software engineers, including DevOps engineers. But I'm also fairly confident in my self-assessment at this point, and I feel I have a good understanding of my strengths and weaknesses, and I feel my strengths are valuable and my weaknesses are manageable. I feel like I should be getting better responses, even in this climate.

I also have no interest in waiting until the market returns to normal (assuming this is not the new normal), or waiting for Applicant Tracking Systems to improve, or relying on references or recruiters; I've had referrals come to nothing too, and I've yet to be contacted by a recruiter that actually represented a genuinely _interesting_ opportunity.

I don't trust self-proclaimed experts or services at this point. I'm on LinkedIn and `r/devops` and see a lot of advice, much of it contradictory. I can accept being wrong – for instance, I've become aware that my résumé should be longer than it was – but I need to _learn_. I cannot accept simply that I know nothing, and that I'm at the mercy of market forces and chance and culture changes. That's not going to feed my wife, my kid, and my cats. I need to understand what is necessary to be hireable.

So I'm working on an experiment: I'm rewriting my résumé in LaTeX so I can easily customize it per job application, then I'll apply to a large number of jobs and see what works and what does not.

There is a narrative in the community that a disproportionate amount of the air right now is being sucked out of the room by candidates who submit 3+-page résumés covering essentially everything they've ever done, not just major projects. Another narrative is that quantified accomplishments are generally made up, which makes sense intuitively. Blame is placed on Applicant Tracking Systems, on AI (at multiple levels), on inept HR departments, on perverse incentives, on the market, etc.

What actually works?

Obviously, I don't have the resources or interest to actually do anything scientifically valid, but I'd like to know more than I do now. Which is evidently very little.

This is getting long, so I might write a followup that goes into the actual process and thinking in more detail. Or I might not, because YOLO.
